\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Preliminaries and Internal Activations}

We build on the stochastic-interpolant formulation used by SiT~\citep{ma2024sit}. Given a data latent $x_0\sim p_{\mathrm{data}}$, Gaussian noise $\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)$, and timestep $t\in[0,1]$, the interpolation path is
\begin{equation}
x_t=\alpha_t x_0+\sigma_t\epsilon ,
\label{eq:stochastic_interpolant}
\end{equation}
with target velocity
\[
u_t=\dot{\alpha}_t x_0+\dot{\sigma}_t\epsilon .
\]
A diffusion transformer $v_\theta$ predicts this velocity from $(x_t,t,c)$ by minimizing
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{FM}}
=
\mathbb{E}_{x_0,\epsilon,t,c}\left[
\left\|v_\theta(x_t,t,c)-u_t\right\|_2^2
\right].
\label{eq:flow_matching_loss}
\end{equation}

Let $A_\ell^t\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times C}$ denote the token activation produced by the diffusion transformer after block $\ell$ when processing $(x_t,t,c)$. Here $N$ is the number of latent tokens and $C$ is the channel dimension. ReAlign uses these internal activations as self-contained alignment targets.

\paragraph{Shared spatial structure.}
Internal layers are not identical, but they often preserve a common spatial scaffold across depth. \Cref{fig:projection_layout_observation} visualizes this with PCA maps and stable-rank diagnostics. This observation motivates a transition interface: instead of directly matching one fixed pair of raw activations, we learn conditional mappings that exploit shared structure while adapting to the representation gap of each layer pair.

\subsection{Conditional Internal Transitions}

Existing internal alignment methods typically match a manually selected pair of layers. In contrast, ReAlign learns a conditional transition function that maps a source activation $A_a^t$ to the representation space of a target layer $b$:
\begin{equation}
\widehat A_{a\to b}^{\,t}
=
G_\psi(A_a^t,e_a,e_b,e_t),
\qquad a<b ,
\label{eq:realign_predictor}
\end{equation}
where $G_\psi$ is a lightweight neural predictor, and $e_a,e_b,e_t$ are source-layer, target-layer, and timestep embeddings. We focus on forward depth transitions from earlier to later layers, since the goal is to expose later-layer representational guidance to earlier computation. The same predictor is shared across valid source--target pairs; layer and timestep conditioning allow it to adapt to different transition gaps.

\paragraph{Transition alignment.}
We train the predictor to approximate the target activation:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}
=
\mathbb{E}_{a<b,t}\left[
d\left(
\widehat A_{a\to b}^{\,t},
\operatorname{sg}(A_b^t)
\right)
\right],
\label{eq:align_loss}
\end{equation}
where $d(\cdot,\cdot)$ is tokenwise cosine distance and $\operatorname{sg}$ stops gradients through the target activation. This term updates the predictor and source-side backbone activations while using the target layer as a stable internal reference.

\subsection{Transition Consistency}

Long-range transitions can be harder to learn directly, especially when the source and target layers are far apart. To stabilize arbitrary-pair prediction, ReAlign encourages direct transitions to agree with compositions of shorter transitions. For $a<b<c$, we define a direct prediction
\begin{equation}
\widehat A_{a\to c}^{\,t}
=
G_\psi(\operatorname{sg}(A_a^t),e_a,e_c,e_t),
\label{eq:direct_transition}
\end{equation}
and an EMA-composed prediction
\begin{equation}
\widehat A_{a\to b\to c}^{\,t}
=
\bar G_\psi\!\left(
\bar G_\psi(\operatorname{sg}(A_a^t),e_a,e_b,e_t),
e_b,e_c,e_t
\right),
\label{eq:composed_target}
\end{equation}
where $\bar G_\psi$ is an EMA copy of the predictor~\citep{tarvainen2017meanteacher}. The consistency loss is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cons}}
=
\mathbb{E}_{a<b<c,t}\left[
d\left(
\widehat A_{a\to c}^{\,t},
\operatorname{sg}(\widehat A_{a\to b\to c}^{\,t})
\right)
\right].
\label{eq:consistency_loss}
\end{equation}
This loss updates the online predictor against a detached composed target, encouraging the shared interface to behave consistently across short and long transitions.

\subsection{Training Objective}

For a small subset of each minibatch, ReAlign feeds the predicted target state to the remaining transformer blocks and trains it with the ordinary flow-matching target. Let $F_{\theta,b:L}$ denote the suffix of the diffusion transformer from block $b$ to the output. The velocity prediction remains a single quantity:
\begin{equation}
\hat v
=
\begin{cases}
F_{\theta,1:L}(x_t,t,c), & \text{standard example},\\
F_{\theta,b:L}(\widehat A_{a\to b}^{\,t},t,c), & \text{FM-path example}.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:fm_path_forward}
\end{equation}
The same flow-matching loss $\|\hat v-u_t\|_2^2$ is applied in both cases, so this path connects transition prediction to the generative objective without introducing a separate output-matching loss.

We also use a lightweight diversity regularizer inspired by DiverseDiT~\citep{yang2026diversedit}. For randomly sampled layer pairs $(i,j)$, independent of the transition pair used in $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}$, we define
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{div}}
=
\mathbb{E}_{i<j,t}
\left[
\operatorname{cos}^2(A_i^t,A_j^t)
\right].
\label{eq:diversity_loss}
\end{equation}
The predictor is intentionally lightweight and regularized, so it acts as a constrained transition interface rather than a replacement for the intervening transformer blocks.

The ReAlign regularizer is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ReAlign}}
=
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}
+
\beta_{\mathrm{cons}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cons}}
+
\beta_{\mathrm{div}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{div}} .
\label{eq:realign_loss}
\end{equation}
The final training objective is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}
=
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{FM}}
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{align}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ReAlign}} .
\label{eq:total_loss}
\end{equation}
Training procedures, predictor capacity, layer-pair sampling, and loss weights are reported in \Cref{app:realign_algorithm_compute,app:realign_hyperparameters}.
