\section{Method}

\subsection{Preliminaries}

We formulate our method within the stochastic-interpolant view adopted by Scalable Interpolant Transformers (SiTs)~\citep{ma2024sit}, which provides a common language for flow-based and diffusion-based generative models. In this view, generation is described as learning a continuous transport process between a simple base distribution and the data distribution. Given a clean sample $x_0$ and a Gaussian noise sample $\epsilon$, an interpolant constructs an intermediate state
\begin{equation}
x_t = \alpha_t x_0 + \sigma_t \epsilon ,
\label{eq:stochastic_interpolant}
\end{equation}
where $t$ denotes time and $\alpha_t,\sigma_t$ determine how much data and noise contribute along the path. For small $t$, the state is close to the data endpoint under common schedules; for large $t$, it approaches the noise endpoint. Sampling then amounts to starting from noise and following a learned vector field back toward the data distribution.

The instantaneous direction of this path is obtained by differentiating the interpolant with respect to time, yielding the target velocity
\begin{equation}
u_t(x_0,\epsilon)
=
\dot{\alpha}_t x_0+\dot{\sigma}_t\epsilon .
\label{eq:interpolant_velocity}
\end{equation}
Since $x_0$ and $\epsilon$ are not available at generation time, a neural network is trained to predict this velocity from the current latent $x_t$, timestep $t$, and optional condition $c$. We denote the SiT backbone by $f_\zeta$ and its velocity head by $v_\zeta$. The standard velocity-matching objective is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{vel}}
=
\mathbb{E}_{x_0,\epsilon,t}
\left[
\left\|
v_\zeta(x_t,t,c)
-
(\dot{\alpha}_t x_0+\dot{\sigma}_t\epsilon)
\right\|_2^2
\right].
\label{eq:flow_matching_loss}
\end{equation}
After training, new samples are generated by drawing an initial noise sample and integrating the learned velocity field in the reverse direction.

\subsection{Internal Representation Consistency}

ReSync is motivated by recent representation-guided training methods. REPA improves diffusion-transformer training by aligning hidden states to external visual representations~\citep{yu2024repa}, while SRA and LayerSync show that useful guidance can also come from the diffusion model itself~\citep{jiang2025sra,haghighi2026layersync}. Our goal is to keep this self-contained property, but to replace direct layer matching with a learnable transition model over depth.

Let a diffusion transformer produce hidden states
\begin{equation}
h_0,h_1,\ldots,h_L,
\label{eq:hidden_sequence}
\end{equation}
where $h_l$ denotes the representation after the $l$-th transformer block. LayerSync-style alignment encourages weaker layers to move toward stronger internal representations. ReSync instead learns how one representation should transform into another. For two layers $a<b$, we introduce a lightweight transition predictor
\begin{equation}
\hat h_b
=
P_\psi(h_a,a,b,t),
\label{eq:resync_predictor}
\end{equation}
which predicts the representation at layer $b$ from the representation at layer $a$, conditioned on the layer indices and timestep. Thus, the auxiliary task is not simply to enforce $h_a\approx h_b$, but to learn the internal transition $h_a\rightarrow h_b$.

\subsection{Layer Alignment Loss}

The first training signal grounds the predicted transition using the real hidden state at the target layer. For a sampled layer pair $a<b$, we define
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{align}}
=
\mathbb{E}_{a<b}
\left[
d\left(
P_\psi(h_a,a,b,t),
\mathrm{sg}(h_b)
\right)
\right],
\label{eq:align_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{sg}(\cdot)$ denotes stop-gradient. We use a tokenwise representation distance; by default,
\begin{equation}
d(u,v)
=
1-\cos(u,v).
\label{eq:cosine_distance}
\end{equation}
This loss has the same grounding role as internal self-alignment, but the alignment is mediated by a learnable transition predictor rather than by directly collapsing two layers together.

\subsection{Internal Consistency Loss}

The key additional constraint is that representation transitions should compose across depth. For three layers $a<b<c$, predicting $c$ directly from $a$ should agree with first predicting $b$ and then continuing from $b$ to $c$:
\begin{equation}
P_\psi(h_a,a,c,t)
\approx
P_\psi(P_\psi(h_a,a,b,t),b,c,t).
\label{eq:transition_composition}
\end{equation}
We implement this with a bootstrap consistency target from a slow-moving EMA copy of the predictor, denoted $\overline{P}_\psi$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cons}}
=
\mathbb{E}_{a<b<c}
\left[
d\left(
P_\psi(P_\psi(h_a,a,b,t),b,c,t),
\mathrm{sg}\left(\overline{P}_\psi(h_a,a,c,t)\right)
\right)
\right].
\label{eq:consistency_loss}
\end{equation}
This is inspired by shortcut-style consistency, but the trajectory is the transformer depth trajectory rather than the sampling-time diffusion trajectory. The consistency term encourages ReSync to learn reusable local transitions that remain compatible when composed into longer jumps.

\subsection{Output Distillation for Layer Skipping}

Hidden-state matching alone may be insufficient: a small error in $\hat h_b$ can be amplified by the remaining transformer blocks. To make the predicted representation useful for generation, we optionally distill the final model output after a skipped-depth forward pass. Let $F_{b+1:L}$ denote the remaining transformer blocks and output head after layer $b$. Given
\begin{equation}
\hat h_b=P_\psi(h_a,a,b,t),
\end{equation}
we resume the backbone from $\hat h_b$ and compute
\begin{equation}
\hat y_{\mathrm{skip}}
=
F_{b+1:L}(\hat h_b,t,c).
\label{eq:skip_output}
\end{equation}
We match this prediction to the full backbone output $\hat y_{\mathrm{full}}$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{out}}
=
\left\|
\hat y_{\mathrm{skip}}
-
\mathrm{sg}(\hat y_{\mathrm{full}})
\right\|_2^2.
\label{eq:output_distillation_loss}
\end{equation}
This optional term connects representation transition learning to the final velocity or denoising prediction, and it provides a natural route to layer-skipping inference through a small transition network.

\subsection{Overall Objective}

The final objective combines the original generative loss with the ReSync auxiliary terms:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}
=
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{gen}}
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{align}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{align}}
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{cons}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cons}}
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{out}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{out}}.
\label{eq:total_loss}
\end{equation}
Here $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{gen}}$ is the original diffusion or flow-matching loss, $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{align}}$ grounds the learned transition with real internal representations, $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cons}}$ enforces compositional consistency across depth, and $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{out}}$ is an optional output-level distillation term for skipped-depth execution.
