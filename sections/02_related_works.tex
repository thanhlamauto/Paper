\section{Related Work}

\noindent\textbf{A central question in recent diffusion research is how intermediate representations should be supervised, aligned, and regularized to improve generative training.} Recent studies have shown that diffusion and flow-based generative models learn meaningful intermediate representations, and that the quality of these representations is closely connected to generative performance \citep{mukhopadhyay2024, kim2025, stracke2025, yu2024repa}. Building on this observation, a prominent line of work improves diffusion training through \emph{representation alignment} with strong pretrained encoders. In particular, REPA aligns diffusion features with external visual representations and shows that such guidance can substantially accelerate convergence and improve generation quality \citep{yu2024repa}. Follow-up work has further strengthened this paradigm through more aggressive alignment strategies and end-to-end training recipes, including HASTE and REPA-E \citep{wang2025haste, leng2025repae}. More recently, iREPA revisits the mechanism behind alignment and argues that its effectiveness is driven less by global semantic information and more by \emph{spatial structure}: generation quality correlates much more strongly with spatial organization in the target representation than with linear-probe accuracy, and simple modifications that better preserve spatial information consistently improve REPA-style training \citep{singh2025irepa}. Despite their strong empirical gains, these approaches rely on external teachers such as DINOv2 and therefore introduce additional training-time overhead, architectural dependency, and reduced portability to scenarios where suitable pretrained encoders are unavailable or less effective \citep{oquab2023dinov2, yu2024repa, singh2025irepa}. 

To reduce this dependence on external supervision, recent self-contained methods instead exploit intrinsic guidance or internal regularization. SRA aligns earlier, noisier student features with later, cleaner features from an EMA teacher constructed from the model itself, showing that diffusion transformers can provide useful guidance without external semantic encoders \citep{jiang2025sra}. VAE-REPA replaces the heavier dual-model setting with a lightweight alignment to off-the-shelf VAE features, while Self-Transcendence uses a staged self-guided pipeline in which internal features are first strengthened and then reused as supervision signals for subsequent training \citep{wang2026vaerepa, sun2026selftranscendence}. In parallel, LayerSync proposes direct self-alignment across depth by using semantically richer deeper layers as intrinsic guidance for weaker shallow layers, whereas Dispersive Loss and DiverseDiT emphasize that effective diffusion transformers also require sufficient diversity across layers or blocks rather than excessive representational similarity \citep{haghighi2026layersync, wang2025dispersive, yang2026diversedit}. Taken together, these works reveal a central tension: some form of cross-layer consistency appears necessary for self-guidance, yet excessive similarity can suppress specialization. Our work is motivated precisely by this gap.
