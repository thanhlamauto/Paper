\section{Empirical Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate the two claims made in the introduction: LARA should improve generation, and its internal representations should become stronger. The main experiments are organized around two questions:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Does LARA improve generation quality across domains and model sizes? (\Cref{sec:image_generation,sec:human_motion_generation})
    \item Does LARA improve the backbone representations it supervises? (\Cref{sec:representation_analysis})
\end{itemize}
The main tables keep comparisons compact. Training settings, sampling protocols, evaluator details, and sensitivity sweeps are reported in \Cref{app:experimental_details,app:realign_ablation_protocols,app:metric_definitions}. Objective and sampling choices are isolated in \Cref{sec:ablation_study}.

\subsection{Image Generation}
\label{sec:image_generation}
\label{sec:experimental_setup}

\paragraph{Implementation details.}
Unless otherwise stated, we follow the class-conditional ImageNet training setup of SiT~\citep{ma2024sit}. We train on ImageNet~\citep{deng2009imagenet} at $256\times256$ resolution using the preprocessing protocol of ADM~\citep{dhariwal2021diffusion}. Each image is encoded by the Stable Diffusion VAE from LDM~\citep{rombach2022ldm} into a latent tensor $z\in\mathbb{R}^{32\times32\times4}$. We evaluate the \texttt{B/2}, \texttt{L/2}, and \texttt{XL/2} SiT configurations with patch size $2$. LARA trains the transition predictor jointly with the backbone using \Cref{eq:total_loss}. Unless specified, we use AdamW with learning rate $10^{-4}$ and batch size $256$. Additional details on architecture, transition-pair sampling, loss weights, cached-transition composition with an EMA target, training length, hardware, and compute budget are organized in \Cref{app:experimental_details}.

\paragraph{Evaluation.}
We report Frechet Inception Distance (FID)~\citep{heusel2017gans}, Inception Score (IS)~\citep{salimans2016improved}, precision, and recall~\citep{kynkaanniemi2019precision} using $50\mathrm{K}$ generated samples. We use the same reference statistics and evaluation implementation for all LARA variants and baselines. Definitions for the diagnostic metrics that are not standard generation benchmarks are provided in \Cref{app:metric_definitions}.

\paragraph{Comparison methods.}
Our main comparisons are against the base SiT backbone and recent self-contained representation-regularized methods: Dispersive Loss~\citep{wang2025dispersive}, SRA~\citep{jiang2025sra}, and LayerSync~\citep{haghighi2026layersync}. For system-level context, we also report representative ImageNet baselines from pixel diffusion~\citep{dhariwal2021diffusion,kingma2023understanding,hoogeboom2023simple,ho2022cascaded}, latent diffusion~\citep{rombach2022ldm}, transformer-hybrid diffusion~\citep{bao2023all,hatamizadeh2024diffit,gao2023mdtv2}, and autoregressive or predictive generation~\citep{tian2024visual,chen2024jepa}. Detailed baseline descriptions and the parameter-matching protocol are deferred to \Cref{app:baseline_descriptions,app:baseline_protocol}.

The no-CFG results show the same pattern across available scales. On \texttt{SiT-B/2}, LARA improves FID from $35.17$ to $25.01$, a $28.9\%$ relative gain over the base model and a $12.6\%$ gain over LayerSync. On \texttt{SiT-L/2}, LARA improves FID from $20.55$ to $11.09$, a $46.0\%$ gain over the base model and a $15.0\%$ gain over LayerSync. On \texttt{SiT-XL/2}, the gains remain consistent as training increases: at $400\mathrm{K}$ iterations, LARA improves FID from $17.32$ to $8.57$; at $1\mathrm{M}$ iterations, from $12.03$ to $6.51$; and at $2\mathrm{M}$ iterations, from $8.85$ to $5.09$. With CFG, LARA achieves the best FID among the SiT-family variants in \Cref{tab:imagenet_cfg}, improving over LayerSync from $1.89$ to $1.84$ while keeping comparable precision and recall.

\subsection{Text-Conditional Human Motion Generation}
\label{sec:human_motion_generation}

To test whether LARA transfers beyond image generation, we additionally evaluate text-conditioned human motion generation on HumanML3D~\citep{guo2022generating}. Given a text description, the model generates a sequence of human poses represented as 3D joint trajectories.

\paragraph{Implementation details.}
We follow the MDM setup~\citep{tevet2022mdm} and use a transformer backbone with the same training budget for the baseline and LARA variant. The main comparison trains MDM with and without LARA for $600\mathrm{K}$ iterations; architecture and loss settings are reported in \Cref{app:realign_hyperparameters}.

\paragraph{Evaluation metrics.}
We report FID and R-Precision (top 3) using the standard HumanML3D evaluation protocol. Definitions and reporting conventions are provided in \Cref{app:metric_definitions}. Lower FID is better, while higher R-Precision indicates better text-motion retrieval alignment.

\paragraph{Results.}
\Cref{tab:humanml3d_results} reports the HumanML3D comparison. This experiment tests whether the same signal-extracting transition objective transfers to a smaller, non-image transformer without changing the generative task.

\subsection{Representation Analysis}
\label{sec:representation_analysis}

Generation metrics show the final effect of training. LARA, however, is designed to act inside the depth hierarchy, so we also inspect hidden states directly. We compare matched-FID checkpoints: an \texttt{SiT-XL/2} trained with LARA for \textbf{[TBD]} iterations and a baseline \texttt{SiT-XL/2} trained for \textbf{[TBD]} iterations. Matching generation quality makes the comparison more direct: differences in the probes reflect how the regularizer changes the learned representations, rather than simply reflecting a stronger final generator.

We probe every backbone block with three layerwise diagnostics: linear classification on Tiny ImageNet~\citep{deng2009imagenet}, linear segmentation on PASCAL VOC~\citep{everingham2010pascal}, and Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) to DINOv2 features~\citep{kornblith2019similarity,oquab2023dinov2}. The two linear probes measure how much semantic and spatial information is exposed by each layer, while CKA measures whether the hidden states organize visual structure similarly to a strong visual representation. Probe heads, layerwise reporting conventions, and additional diagnostics are reported in \Cref{app:layer_statistics_projection,app:metric_definitions}.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{figures/representation_analysis_placeholder.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Layerwise representation quality.} Schematic placeholder for matched-FID probes across backbone depth: Tiny ImageNet linear classification, PASCAL VOC linear segmentation, and DINOv2 CKA. The measured curves and gains are filled after running the appendix protocol; the intended readout is whether LARA (Ours) exposes stronger weak- and middle-layer representations than the baseline under the same generation-quality setting.}
    \label{fig:representation_analysis}
\end{figure}

\Cref{fig:representation_analysis} summarizes the layerwise probes and suggests two main trends. First, LARA distributes useful features more evenly across depth: averaged over the evaluated layers, linear classification improves by \textbf{[TBD]\%}, segmentation mIoU improves by \textbf{[TBD]\%}, and CKA alignment with DINOv2 improves by \textbf{[TBD]\%}. This is consistent with the role of the transition predictor: supervision is no longer concentrated around one selected pair, but reused across many source--target depths. Second, the best layer also improves, with peak linear accuracy increasing by \textbf{[TBD]\%}. This is less automatic than raising weak layers toward a fixed reference. It indicates that shared transition learning can reorganize the feature hierarchy, producing a stronger representation rather than merely copying one high-performing block.

This pattern is the internal trace expected from LARA. Weak layers expose cleaner signals, middle layers become stronger targets, and the gains spread across the hierarchy instead of collapsing around a hand-picked reference layer.

The sweep keeps the default training timestep sampler uniform. Logit-normal timestep samplers are included only as controls, while the remaining panels tune the three LARA loss weights one at a time around the default configuration.

We choose the layer-pair sampler by first asking where the simplest strategy fails. A temporary diagnostic run samples source layers, target layers, and timesteps uniformly, then groups transition-prediction error by normalized layer gap, source depth, and timestep range. The purpose is not to make uniform sampling the final recipe; it is to identify which internal transitions are too easy, too unstable, or useful for supervision.

The resulting strategy is simple. Adjacent layers usually have low error, so repeatedly sampling them mostly teaches a local copy task. Very distant transitions have high error and can inject noisy auxiliary gradients early in training. The useful regime lies between these extremes: medium layer gaps with broad source-depth coverage. We therefore keep the training timestep sampler uniform, preserve coverage across valid depths for layer-agnostic alignment, and shift layer-pair probability mass away from trivial adjacent pairs toward informative medium-gap transitions. The full binned diagnostic table and schedule details are reported in \Cref{app:uniform_transition_diagnostic,app:realign_ablation_protocols}.

Overall, the experiments indicate that LARA's gains are not simply a consequence of choosing a better weak--strong pair. LayerSync shows that a selected internal alignment can regularize training, but its supervision remains tied to one hand-picked relation. LARA uses the same internal hierarchy more broadly: the shared transition predictor learns from many source--target pairs, while layer conditioning preserves the pair-specific gaps. This gives the model a more global alignment signal across depth, rather than a heuristic constraint anchored to a single reference layer.
