\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate the two claims made in the introduction: ReAlign should improve generation, and its internal representations should become stronger. The main experiments are organized around two questions:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Does ReAlign improve generation quality and training efficiency across domains and model sizes? (\Cref{sec:image_generation,sec:human_motion_generation})
    \item Does ReAlign improve the backbone representations it supervises? (\Cref{sec:representation_analysis})
\end{itemize}
The main tables keep comparisons compact. Training settings, sampling protocols, evaluator details, and sensitivity sweeps are reported in \Cref{app:experimental_details,app:realign_ablation_protocols,app:metric_definitions}. Objective and sampling choices are isolated in \Cref{sec:ablation_study}.

\subsection{Image Generation}
\label{sec:image_generation}
\label{sec:experimental_setup}

\paragraph{Implementation details.}
Unless otherwise stated, we follow the class-conditional ImageNet training setup of SiT~\citep{ma2024sit}. We train on ImageNet~\citep{deng2009imagenet} at $256\times256$ resolution using the preprocessing protocol of ADM~\citep{dhariwal2021diffusion}. Each image is encoded by the Stable Diffusion VAE from LDM~\citep{rombach2022ldm} into a latent tensor $z\in\mathbb{R}^{32\times32\times4}$. We evaluate the \texttt{B/2}, \texttt{L/2}, and \texttt{XL/2} SiT configurations with patch size $2$. ReAlign trains the transition predictor jointly with the backbone using \Cref{eq:total_loss}. Unless specified, we use AdamW with learning rate $10^{-4}$ and batch size $256$. Additional details on architecture, transition-pair sampling, EMA decay, loss weights, training length, hardware, and compute budget are organized in \Cref{app:experimental_details}.

\paragraph{Evaluation.}
We report Frechet Inception Distance (FID)~\citep{heusel2017gans}, Inception Score (IS)~\citep{salimans2016improved}, precision, and recall~\citep{kynkaanniemi2019precision} using $50\mathrm{K}$ generated samples. We use the same reference statistics and evaluation implementation for all ReAlign variants and baselines. Definitions for the diagnostic metrics that are not standard generation benchmarks are provided in \Cref{app:metric_definitions}.

\paragraph{Comparison methods.}
Our main comparisons are against the base SiT/DiT backbone and recent representation-regularized training methods. For self-contained guidance, we compare with Dispersive Loss~\citep{wang2025dispersive}, SRA~\citep{jiang2025sra}, and LayerSync~\citep{haghighi2026layersync}. For externally guided training, we compare with REPA~\citep{yu2024repa} and REED~\citep{wang2025flexible}. For completeness, we also report representative image-generation baselines: pixel-space diffusion models including ADM~\citep{dhariwal2021diffusion}, VDM++~\citep{kingma2023understanding}, Simple Diffusion~\citep{hoogeboom2023simple}, and CDM~\citep{ho2022cascaded}; latent diffusion and transformer-based models including LDM~\citep{rombach2022ldm}, U-ViT-H/2~\citep{bao2023all}, DiffiT~\citep{hatamizadeh2024diffit}, MDTv2-XL/2~\citep{gao2023mdtv2}, MaskDiT~\citep{zheng2023fast}, SD-DiT~\citep{zhu2024sddit}, DiT~\citep{peebles2023scalable}, and SiT~\citep{ma2024sit}; and autoregressive or predictive-generation baselines such as VAR~\citep{tian2024visual} and D-JEPA~\citep{chen2024jepa}. Detailed baseline descriptions and the parameter-matching protocol are deferred to \Cref{app:baseline_descriptions,app:baseline_protocol}.

\subsection{Text-Conditional Human Motion Generation}
\label{sec:human_motion_generation}

To test whether ReAlign transfers beyond image generation, we additionally evaluate text-conditioned human motion generation on HumanML3D~\citep{guo2022generating}. Given a text description, the model generates a sequence of human poses represented as 3D joint trajectories.

\paragraph{Implementation details.}
We follow the MDM setup~\citep{tevet2022mdm} and use a transformer backbone with the same training budget for the baseline and ReAlign variant. The main comparison trains MDM with and without ReAlign for $600\mathrm{K}$ iterations; architecture and loss settings are reported in \Cref{app:realign_hyperparameters}.

\paragraph{Evaluation metrics.}
We report FID and R-Precision (top 3) using the standard HumanML3D evaluation protocol. Definitions and reporting conventions are provided in \Cref{app:metric_definitions}. Lower FID is better, while higher R-Precision indicates better text-motion retrieval alignment.

\paragraph{Results.}
\Cref{tab:humanml3d_results} reports the HumanML3D comparison. This experiment tests whether the same signal-extracting transition objective transfers to a smaller, non-image transformer without changing the generative task.

\subsection{Representation Analysis}
\label{sec:representation_analysis}

Generation metrics show the final effect of training. ReAlign, however, is meant to act inside the depth hierarchy, so we also inspect hidden states directly. We compare matched-FID checkpoints: an \texttt{SiT-XL/2} trained with ReAlign for \textbf{[TBD]} epochs and a baseline \texttt{SiT-XL/2} trained for \textbf{[TBD]} epochs. This keeps sample quality comparable while exposing how each model organizes intermediate features.

We probe every backbone block with three layerwise diagnostics: linear classification on Tiny ImageNet~\citep{deng2009imagenet}, linear segmentation on PASCAL VOC~\citep{everingham2010pascal}, and Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) to DINOv2 features~\citep{kornblith2019similarity,oquab2023dinov2}. The two linear probes measure how much semantic and spatial information is exposed by each layer, while CKA measures whether the hidden states organize visual structure similarly to a strong visual representation. Probe heads, layerwise reporting conventions, and additional diagnostics are reported in \Cref{app:layer_statistics_projection,app:metric_definitions}.

This pattern is the internal trace expected from a shared transition predictor. ReAlign creates useful interfaces across depth; weak layers expose cleaner signals, middle layers become better targets, and the final model benefits without external teacher supervision.
