\section{Empirical Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We organize the experiments around two comparisons. First, we compare generation quality under matched backbones and training budgets to assess whether a shared layer-aware transition predictor improves optimization without external representation models. Second, we examine intermediate features to see whether the internal representation hierarchy changes in the expected direction. We therefore begin with ImageNet generation, test transfer to motion generation, and then probe intermediate features directly. The main tables keep the comparison compact; training settings, sampling protocols, evaluator details, and extended sensitivity sweeps are reported in \Cref{app:experimental_details,app:realign_ablation_protocols,app:metric_definitions}. The design choices behind the final recipe are isolated in \Cref{sec:ablation_study}.

\subsection{Image Generation}
\label{sec:image_generation}
\label{sec:experimental_setup}

\paragraph{Implementation details.}
Unless otherwise stated, we follow the class-conditional ImageNet training setup of SiT~\citep{ma2024sit}. We train on ImageNet~\citep{deng2009imagenet} at $256\times256$ resolution using the preprocessing protocol of ADM~\citep{dhariwal2021diffusion}. Each image is encoded by the Stable Diffusion VAE from LDM~\citep{rombach2022ldm} into a latent tensor $z\in\mathbb{R}^{32\times32\times4}$. We evaluate the \texttt{B/2}, \texttt{L/2}, and \texttt{XL/2} SiT configurations with patch size $2$. LARA trains the transition predictor jointly with the backbone using \Cref{eq:total_loss}. Unless specified, we use AdamW with learning rate $10^{-4}$ and batch size $256$. Additional details on architecture, transition-pair sampling, loss weights, cached-transition composition with an EMA target, training length, hardware, and compute budget are organized in \Cref{app:experimental_details}.

We use the same reference statistics and evaluation implementation for all LARA variants and baselines. Definitions for the diagnostic metrics that are not standard generation benchmarks are provided in \Cref{app:metric_definitions}.

Our controlled comparisons use the same SiT backbone and training budget as the base model, and compare against the self-contained internal baselines reported in \Cref{tab:imagenet_no_cfg}: Dispersive Loss~\citep{wang2025dispersive}, SRA~\citep{jiang2025sra}, and LayerSync~\citep{haghighi2026layersync}.

We then include a CFG-based system comparison to place the final model among standard ImageNet generators, where guidance can change the quality--diversity trade-off~\citep{kynkaanniemi2024guidance}.

\textbf{Scaling across backbones.} LARA consistently improves the SiT backbone under matched model size and training budget. On \texttt{SiT-B/2}, FID decreases from $35.17$ to $25.01$, improving over both the vanilla model and the strongest internal baseline. The same trend holds as capacity increases: \texttt{SiT-L/2} improves from $20.55$ to $11.09$, while \texttt{SiT-XL/2} improves from $17.32$ to $8.57$ at $400\mathrm{K}$. The gains remain stable at longer training budgets, reaching $6.51$ at $1\mathrm{M}$ and $5.09$ at $2\mathrm{M}$. With CFG, LARA also improves over LayerSync from $1.93$ to $1.88$ FID while maintaining comparable precision and recall. These results indicate that the learned transition signal scales beyond a single hand-picked alignment pair.

\subsection{Text-Conditional Human Motion Generation}
\label{sec:human_motion_generation}

To test whether LARA transfers beyond image generation, we additionally evaluate text-conditioned human motion generation on HumanML3D~\citep{guo2020action2motion,guo2022generating}. Given a text description, the model generates a sequence of human poses represented as 3D joint trajectories.

\paragraph{Implementation details.}
We follow the MDM setup~\citep{tevet2022mdm} and use the same motion-transformer backbone for all rows. MDM and LayerSync are trained for $600\mathrm{K}$ iterations, while LARA is evaluated at $300\mathrm{K}$ and $400\mathrm{K}$ checkpoints; architecture and loss settings are reported in \Cref{app:realign_hyperparameters}.

\paragraph{Evaluation metrics.}
We report FID and R-Precision (top 3) using the standard HumanML3D evaluation protocol. Definitions and reporting conventions are provided in \Cref{app:metric_definitions}. Lower FID is better, while higher R-Precision indicates better text-motion retrieval alignment.

\paragraph{Results.}
\Cref{tab:humanml3d_results} tests whether the same transition signal transfers to a smaller, non-image transformer without changing the generative task.

\textbf{Transfer beyond image latents.} LARA improves both HumanML3D metrics despite using only $300\mathrm{K}$ training iterations, while MDM and LayerSync use $600\mathrm{K}$. It reduces FID from $0.503$ to $0.4343$ and increases R-Precision from $0.7202$ to $0.7675$ relative to MDM. It also outperforms LayerSync's $0.4801$ FID and $0.7434$ R-Precision with half the training budget. This suggests that LARA is not specialized to spatial image latents; the same transition objective can regularize a motion transformer whose hierarchy is induced by temporal pose dynamics.

\subsection{Representation Analysis}
\label{sec:representation_analysis}

\textbf{Representation quality.} Generation metrics measure the final samples, but LARA acts inside the transformer hierarchy. We therefore probe \texttt{SiT-XL/2} hidden states from the SiT baseline at $2\mathrm{M}$ iterations, LayerSync at $800\mathrm{K}$, and LARA at $500\mathrm{K}$. The probes test complementary properties: Tiny ImageNet classification measures semantic exposure, PASCAL VOC segmentation measures spatial localization, and DINOv2 CKA measures alignment with strong visual features. Probe heads, layerwise reporting conventions, and additional diagnostics are reported in \Cref{app:layer_statistics_projection,app:metric_definitions}.

\textbf{Layerwise behavior.}
\Cref{fig:representation_analysis} shows that LARA strengthens the hidden hierarchy even under a smaller training budget. LARA is evaluated at $500\mathrm{K}$ iterations, whereas LayerSync uses $800\mathrm{K}$ and the SiT baseline uses $2\mathrm{M}$. Averaged over layers, LARA improves over SiT by $54.8\%$ on Tiny ImageNet classification, $113.6\%$ on PASCAL VOC segmentation, and $155.6\%$ on DINOv2-g CKA. It also improves over LayerSync by $20.9\%$, $21.6\%$, and $9.5\%$, respectively. The best layer improves as well, reaching $55.71$ classification accuracy, $47.15$ segmentation mIoU, and $0.287$ CKA.

The shape of the curves gives a more useful diagnostic than the averages alone. LayerSync raises representation quality around the region influenced by its selected alignment pair, which produces a relatively sharp layerwise profile. LARA instead spreads the improvement across a wider portion of the network. This is expected from an adaptive transition objective: the predictor is trained on many source--target pairs, so the supervision is not concentrated on one manually chosen path. The resulting representations are stronger, smoother across depth, and less dependent on a single reference layer, suggesting that LARA shares useful structure while preserving the different semantic roles of individual blocks.

\textbf{Predictor-output behavior.}
\Cref{fig:predictor_output_behavior} inspects whether the predicted target states resemble the actual target-layer activations, rather than only reducing a scalar loss. Across layer depths and denoising timesteps, the useful diagnostic is whether $\widehat A_{a\to b}^{\,t}$ recovers the spatial layout and semantic level of $A_b^t$ while still being conditioned on the source layer $a$. This directly probes the mechanism behind the smoother layerwise profile: LARA should learn reusable transitions into many target-layer coordinate systems, not a set of isolated layer copies.

\section{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:ablation_study}

\textbf{Ablation overview.} We design the ablations around the main alternative explanations for LARA's gains. The objective could work only as generic regularization, depend on delicate loss weights, require an oversized predictor, or benefit from a fortunate layer-pair sampler. Each control changes one factor while keeping the backbone, training budget, and evaluation protocol fixed whenever possible. Extended controls are reported in \Cref{app:realign_ablation_protocols}.

\textbf{Objective components.} To separate transition learning from auxiliary regularization, \Cref{tab:realign_ablation} ablates each loss term. The diversity-only variant improves over the vanilla baseline but remains close to LayerSync, showing that feature decorrelation alone cannot explain the full gain. Adding the layer-conditioned transition predictor already improves over fixed-pair alignment, and consistency further lowers FID by tying direct and composed transitions. The full objective performs best, indicating that LARA benefits from both reusable transition prediction and cross-depth consistency.

\textbf{Loss weights and timestep sampler.} We keep the main ablation focused on the design choices that determine the final recipe. The full loss-weight and timestep-sampler sweep is reported in \Cref{tab:main_hyperparameter_tuning}.

\textbf{Layer-pair sampling.} \Cref{tab:pair_sampling_strategy_fid} separates two choices: how far apart the source and target layers are, and where that pair sits in the network. Full uniform already improves over LayerSync because LARA trains over many pairs rather than one fixed pair. Depth-center only improves further by placing more pairs around the middle of the hierarchy while still sampling varied gaps. Depth-centered gap sampling performs best by combining this centered pair location with a medium-gap bias, giving $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}$ cleaner supervision while still covering many source--target pairs.
