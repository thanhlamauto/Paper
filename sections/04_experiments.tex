\section{Empirical Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

The experiments test the two claims behind LARA. First, a shared layer-aware transition predictor should improve generation without changing the backbone or relying on an external representation model. Second, if the objective acts through the intended mechanism, the improvement should also be visible in the internal representation hierarchy. We therefore begin with ImageNet generation, test transfer to motion generation, and then probe intermediate features directly. The main tables keep the comparison compact; training settings, sampling protocols, evaluator details, and extended sensitivity sweeps are reported in \Cref{app:experimental_details,app:realign_ablation_protocols,app:metric_definitions}. The design choices behind the final recipe are isolated in \Cref{sec:ablation_study}.

\subsection{Image Generation}
\label{sec:image_generation}
\label{sec:experimental_setup}

\paragraph{Implementation details.}
Unless otherwise stated, we follow the class-conditional ImageNet training setup of SiT~\citep{ma2024sit}. We train on ImageNet~\citep{deng2009imagenet} at $256\times256$ resolution using the preprocessing protocol of ADM~\citep{dhariwal2021diffusion}. Each image is encoded by the Stable Diffusion VAE from LDM~\citep{rombach2022ldm} into a latent tensor $z\in\mathbb{R}^{32\times32\times4}$. We evaluate the \texttt{B/2}, \texttt{L/2}, and \texttt{XL/2} SiT configurations with patch size $2$. LARA trains the transition predictor jointly with the backbone using \Cref{eq:total_loss}. Unless specified, we use AdamW with learning rate $10^{-4}$ and batch size $256$. Additional details on architecture, transition-pair sampling, loss weights, cached-transition composition with an EMA target, training length, hardware, and compute budget are organized in \Cref{app:experimental_details}.

\paragraph{Evaluation.}
We report Frechet Inception Distance (FID)~\citep{heusel2017gans}, Inception Score (IS)~\citep{salimans2016improved,szegedy2016rethinking}, precision, and recall~\citep{kynkaanniemi2019precision} using $50\mathrm{K}$ generated samples. We use the same reference statistics and evaluation implementation for all LARA variants and baselines. Definitions for the diagnostic metrics that are not standard generation benchmarks are provided in \Cref{app:metric_definitions}.

\paragraph{Comparison methods.}
Our main comparisons are against the base SiT backbone and recent self-contained representation-regularized methods: Dispersive Loss~\citep{wang2025dispersive}, SRA~\citep{jiang2025sra}, and LayerSync~\citep{haghighi2026layersync}. For system-level context, we also report representative ImageNet baselines from pixel diffusion~\citep{dhariwal2021diffusion,kingma2023understanding,hoogeboom2023simple,ho2022cascaded}, latent diffusion~\citep{rombach2022ldm}, transformer-hybrid diffusion~\citep{bao2023all,hatamizadeh2024diffit,gao2023mdtv2}, and autoregressive or predictive generation~\citep{tian2024visual,chen2024jepa}. Detailed baseline descriptions and the parameter-matching protocol are deferred to \Cref{app:baseline_descriptions,app:baseline_protocol}.

\paragraph{Results.}
ImageNet provides the main testbed because it exposes both optimization quality and scaling behavior. We compare methods within the same SiT backbone and training budget, so changes in FID can be attributed to the auxiliary alignment objective rather than to model size, sampler, or guidance. We then include a CFG-based system comparison to place the final model among standard ImageNet generators, where guidance can change the quality--diversity trade-off~\citep{kynkaanniemi2024guidance}.

The improvement is not confined to one backbone or training length. On \texttt{SiT-B/2}, LARA reduces FID from $35.17$ to $25.01$, a $28.9\%$ gain over the base model and a $12.6\%$ gain over LayerSync. On \texttt{SiT-L/2}, FID improves from $20.55$ to $11.09$. The same pattern remains on \texttt{SiT-XL/2} as training increases: $17.32\!\to\!8.57$ at $400\mathrm{K}$, $12.03\!\to\!6.51$ at $1\mathrm{M}$, and $8.85\!\to\!5.09$ at $2\mathrm{M}$. With CFG, LARA also improves over LayerSync from $1.89$ to $1.82$ while maintaining comparable precision and recall. The consistent gains support the central claim that layer-aware transition supervision scales beyond a single hand-picked alignment pair.

\subsection{Text-Conditional Human Motion Generation}
\label{sec:human_motion_generation}

To test whether LARA transfers beyond image generation, we additionally evaluate text-conditioned human motion generation on HumanML3D~\citep{guo2020action2motion,guo2022generating}. Given a text description, the model generates a sequence of human poses represented as 3D joint trajectories.

\paragraph{Implementation details.}
We follow the MDM setup~\citep{tevet2022mdm} and use a transformer backbone with the same training budget for the baseline and LARA variant. The main comparison trains MDM with and without LARA for $600\mathrm{K}$ iterations; architecture and loss settings are reported in \Cref{app:realign_hyperparameters}.

\paragraph{Evaluation metrics.}
We report FID and R-Precision (top 3) using the standard HumanML3D evaluation protocol. Definitions and reporting conventions are provided in \Cref{app:metric_definitions}. Lower FID is better, while higher R-Precision indicates better text-motion retrieval alignment.

\paragraph{Results.}
HumanML3D tests whether the same transition signal transfers to a smaller, non-image transformer without changing the generative task.

\subsection{Representation Analysis}
\label{sec:representation_analysis}

Generation metrics only show the final outcome. Since LARA acts inside the depth hierarchy, we also ask whether the learned hidden states become stronger. We use matched-FID checkpoints--an \texttt{SiT-XL/2} trained with LARA for \textbf{[TBD]} iterations and a baseline \texttt{SiT-XL/2} trained for \textbf{[TBD]} iterations--so probe differences reflect representation organization rather than a trivial gap in sample quality.

We probe every backbone block with three layerwise diagnostics: linear classification on Tiny ImageNet~\citep{deng2009imagenet}, linear segmentation on PASCAL VOC~\citep{everingham2010pascal}, and Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) to DINOv2 features~\citep{kornblith2019similarity,oquab2023dinov2}. The two linear probes measure how much semantic and spatial information is exposed by each layer, while CKA measures whether the hidden states organize visual structure similarly to a strong visual representation. Probe heads, layerwise reporting conventions, and additional diagnostics are reported in \Cref{app:layer_statistics_projection,app:metric_definitions}.

\section{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:ablation_study}

The ablations are organized around the possible failure modes of the method. A transition objective could help only because it adds extra regularization, require delicate loss weights, depend on an oversized predictor, or benefit from a fortunate layer-pair sampler. We test these alternatives in sequence, keeping the main recipe fixed unless the corresponding component is under study. Extended controls are reported in \Cref{app:realign_ablation_protocols}.

Once the objective is fixed, the remaining concern is whether it depends on a narrow tuning window. The selected setting uses a uniform timestep sampler and moderate loss weights; logit-normal timestep samplers are included as controls rather than as part of the final recipe. The one-at-a-time sweeps keep the interpretation local to each component and are meant to verify that LARA does not rely on a fragile hyperparameter choice.

The sweep uses a compact ConvNet predictor. This choice follows the same spatial intuition as i-REPA~\citep{singh2025irepa}: SiT latents keep a grid layout, so local convolutional mixing can capture shared perceptual structure between layers while adding few parameters. Capacity then controls a separate trade-off. A very small predictor cannot match the geometry of different target layers and gives weak transition supervision. A very large predictor can instead memorize layer representations, weakening the regularization signal while adding overhead. We therefore use a moderate predictor and treat roughly $5$--$10\%$ of the backbone size as the practical range.

Sampling also affects performance through the layer gap, the source layer, and the timestep schedule. We choose the final sampler from a uniform diagnostic over these axes: it favors informative medium-gap transitions, keeps broad source-depth coverage, and leaves the training timestep sampler uniform. The full diagnostic and schedule are given in \Cref{app:uniform_transition_diagnostic,app:realign_ablation_protocols}.

Together, the ablations indicate that LARA's gains are not a consequence of choosing a better weak--strong pair. LayerSync shows that selected internal alignment can regularize training, but its supervision remains tied to one relation. LARA uses the same hierarchy more broadly: the shared predictor learns from many source--target pairs, while layer conditioning preserves their pair-specific gaps. This replaces a single-reference constraint with a smoother and more coherent alignment signal across depth.
