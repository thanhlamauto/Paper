\section{Introduction}

Diffusion and flow-matching transformers have achieved strong generative performance, but training them efficiently remains challenging at scale~\citep{ho2020ddpm,song2021sde,lipman2022flow,peebles2023scalable,ma2024sit}. A recent line of work improves training through representation alignment, where intermediate diffusion features are encouraged to match informative target representations~\citep{yu2024repa,jiang2025sra,haghighi2026layersync}.

Most alignment methods fall into two categories. External alignment methods use pretrained visual encoders such as DINOv2 or vision-language models as targets~\citep{yu2024repa,oquab2023dinov2,wang2025haste,leng2025repae,wang2026vaerepa,wang2024qwen2vl}. These targets provide useful semantic guidance, but introduce additional dependencies on external representation models. Internal alignment methods instead derive supervision from the diffusion transformer itself. LayerSync directly matches selected internal layers~\citep{haghighi2026layersync}, while related self-alignment methods use projection modules or EMA teachers to reduce mismatch between internal states~\citep{jiang2025sra,tarvainen2017meanteacher}.

The remaining difficulty is layer-pair selection. Internal representations vary substantially across depth, and the transition gap from one layer pair can differ from another. A pair that gives a useful signal for one source--target transition may be poorly conditioned for another, so fixed-pair alignment still requires careful manual choices and may not transfer cleanly across model depths.

We propose \emph{ReAlign}, a self-contained representation-alignment framework based on conditional transition prediction. Rather than directly forcing two selected layers to be similar, ReAlign learns a lightweight predictor that maps an activation from a source layer to a target layer conditioned on the source index, target index, and denoising timestep. This lets one predictor support arbitrary layer pairs while accounting for pair-specific representation gaps. The key design point is that diffusion-transformer layers differ across depth, yet preserve shared spatial structure that a conditional transition interface can exploit. We further add a path-consistency objective so direct long-range transitions agree with compositions of shorter transitions.

Across ImageNet $256\times256$ experiments, ReAlign improves over the base SiT model and internal alignment baselines under the same training budget. For example, with SiT-B/2 and no classifier-free guidance, ReAlign reduces FID from $25.78$ for the baseline and $21.32$ for LayerSync to $18.88$. Representation probes, objective ablations, pair-sampling diagnostics, and overhead analysis support the same conclusion: conditional internal transitions provide a flexible self-contained alignment signal with small training-time cost.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=0.25em,itemsep=0.15em,parsep=0pt]
    \item We formulate internal representation alignment for diffusion transformers as conditional transition prediction across layers.
    \item We introduce a pair-conditioned predictor that supports arbitrary source--target layer pairs, reducing reliance on manually selected alignments.
    \item We add a transition-consistency objective that stabilizes long-range prediction by matching direct transitions with composed shorter transitions.
    \item We show that ReAlign improves diffusion transformer training over direct internal-alignment baselines with small training-time overhead.
\end{itemize}
