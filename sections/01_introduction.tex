\section{Introduction}

Diffusion and flow-matching models have become a standard route to high-fidelity generation~\citep{ho2020ddpm,song2019score,song2021sde,lipman2022flow}. Transformer backbones such as DiT and SiT make these models scalable, but training them remains expensive~\citep{peebles2023scalable,ma2024sit}. A growing line of work improves training by shaping the hidden representations learned inside the diffusion transformer.

Representation guidance can be external or internal. External methods align diffusion-transformer hidden states with features from pretrained encoders such as DINOv2 or vision-language models~\citep{yu2024repa,wang2025haste,leng2025repae,wang2026vaerepa,oquab2023dinov2,wang2024qwen2vl}. These targets are often useful, but they add computation, couple the training recipe to a particular teacher, and become less portable when a strong pretrained encoder is unavailable. Internal methods avoid this dependency by using the diffusion transformer's own depth hierarchy as supervision~\citep{jiang2025sra,haghighi2026layersync}.

The main difficulty is that internal layers are not interchangeable. Prior analyses show that representation quality varies across depth and often peaks before final decoder-like blocks~\citep{mukhopadhyay2024,kim2025,stracke2025,haghighi2026layersync}. Direct internal alignment therefore needs to decide which weak and strong layers should be matched, and that choice is usually heuristic, architecture-dependent, and sensitive to model depth. It also assumes that the selected hidden states can be matched directly in their raw coordinates, despite representation mismatch across layers.

We propose \emph{ReSync}, a lightweight layer-aware transition predictor for flexible cross-depth internal alignment. Instead of aligning one manually selected pair of layers, ReSync samples source--target layer pairs and learns a shared predictor that maps an earlier hidden state into the representation space of a later layer. The predictor is conditioned on the source layer, target layer, and denoising timestep, so the same module can support arbitrary layer pairs without training a separate adapter for each pair. In this framing, the useful object is the cross-layer transition, not a particular pair of endpoints.

This view makes the method simple: ReSync does not force two layers to become identical. It learns a bridge between their representation spaces. Fixed-pair alignment supervises selected endpoints; ReSync supervises the relation that carries information across depth. Sharing this bridge across many cross-layer transitions spreads supervision through the model and encourages a common transition pattern, while a lightweight consistency regularizer keeps short and long transitions coordinated. We also use stochastic layer-pair training; uniform sampling already gives a strong baseline, and a pair-selection strategy based on transition difficulty can further bias training toward informative but learnable pairs.

Empirically, ReSync improves generation under matched training settings; on ImageNet $256\times256$ with SiT-B/2 and no classifier-free guidance, it improves FID from $25.78$ for the baseline and $21.32$ for LayerSync to $18.88$. The experiments evaluate scaling across model sizes, objective ablations, pair-sampling ablations, representation probes, and overhead. Together these results support the central claim: a shared layer-aware transition predictor makes internal layer alignment less heuristic, more flexible across depth, and more useful for diffusion transformer representation learning.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=0.25em,itemsep=0.15em,parsep=0pt]
    \item We introduce a lightweight layer-aware transition predictor for flexible cross-depth internal alignment in diffusion transformers.
    \item We use stochastic layer-pair training and a simple pair-selection strategy to reduce reliance on hand-crafted layer choices.
    \item We provide generation, scaling, ablation, representation, and overhead analyses showing that ReSync improves sample quality and internal representations with minimal standard-sampling cost.
\end{itemize}
