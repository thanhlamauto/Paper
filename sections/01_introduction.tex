\section{Introduction}

Denoising generative models, including diffusion models~\citep{ho2020ddpm,song2019score,song2021sde} and flow matching models~\citep{lipman2022flow}, have become a dominant approach for high-fidelity generation. Transformer-based backbones such as DiT and SiT further improve scalability, but they also make training efficiency a central bottleneck~\citep{peebles2023scalable,ma2024sit}. This has motivated a growing line of work that seeks to improve diffusion transformers by improving the representations they learn during training.

Recent studies show that intermediate representations are closely tied to both optimization and generation quality~\citep{mukhopadhyay2024,kim2025,stracke2025,yu2024repa}. A successful strategy is to align diffusion-transformer hidden states with semantic features from external teachers, such as DINOv2 or large vision-language models~\citep{yu2024repa,wang2025haste,leng2025repae,wang2026vaerepa,oquab2023dinov2,wang2024qwen2vl}. More recent analysis in iREPA suggests that preserving spatial structure can be important beyond global semantic alignment, and reports improvements to REPA-style training by modifying the projection and normalization used for alignment~\citep{singh2025irepa}. While effective, this paradigm depends on additional pretrained models, adds training-time overhead, and may inherit teacher-specific biases in domains where strong external encoders are unavailable or mismatched.

These limitations motivate self-contained guidance, where the diffusion transformer supplies its own training signal. This direction is supported by two observations. First, diffusion-transformer features exhibit a depthwise representation hierarchy: linear-probe and CKA-style analyses with DINOv2 features show that semantic quality improves across intermediate blocks and often peaks before decoder-like final layers~\citep{lecun2015deep,mukhopadhyay2024,kim2025,stracke2025,oquab2023dinov2,haghighi2026layersync}. Second, external representation guidance often provides stronger benefits when applied to weaker or earlier parts of the network, suggesting that internal supervision can be most valuable when it improves underdeveloped representations~\citep{yu2024repa,wang2025haste}. Together, these observations suggest that the model's stronger internal states can provide useful guidance for its weaker ones, without requiring an external encoder.

Existing self-contained methods improve diffusion transformers by regularizing internal representations, but they mostly operate on hidden states as static objects. Dispersive-style losses encourage hidden features to remain diverse~\citep{wang2025dispersive,yang2026diversedit}, while SRA and LayerSync align selected internal states across depth or noise levels~\citep{jiang2025sra,haghighi2026layersync}. These objectives demonstrate that useful guidance already exists inside the diffusion transformer. However, they leave a different question open: instead of deciding which hidden states should be close or far apart, can the model learn an interface that exposes recoverable signals between them and reuses those signals across many layers rather than a fixed pair?

Projection heads commonly used in representation alignment provide an additional clue. REPA maps noisy diffusion-transformer hidden states through a trainable projection head before matching them to clean pretrained-encoder features, using the head as an interface for distilling noise-invariant semantic targets~\citep{yu2024repa}. SRA similarly projects earlier or noisier student features before aligning them with later or cleaner EMA-teacher features, and motivates this design as a softer alignment that avoids directly disrupting the generative role of each layer and timestep~\citep{jiang2025sra}. These designs suggest that raw hidden states from different sources or layers should not always be matched directly. Rather than treating each projector as a local adapter for one selected pair, we ask whether a small conditional module can extract the target-layer signal that an earlier layer is already informative enough to recover. In this view, projection is not only a way to distill intermediate knowledge; it can also filter layer-specific mismatch, so that supervision reaches weaker earlier layers in a more usable form. This follows the virtuous-cycle view of LayerSync, where stronger internal layers guide weaker ones and improved early features then support stronger later representations, but replaces direct self-alignment with transition-mediated prediction~\citep{haghighi2026layersync}.

To this end, we propose \emph{ReSync}, a lightweight self-distillation objective that uses a shared timestep-conditioned predictor to route useful internal supervision across depth. By predicting later-layer states from earlier-layer states, ReSync exposes recoverable signals to many source layers; a compositional consistency constraint further ties short and long transitions without external encoders or additional data.

Our main contributions are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=0.25em,itemsep=0.15em,parsep=0pt]
    \item We identify direct hidden-state matching as a rigid form of internal guidance and recast projection-based alignment as a signal-extracting, mismatch-filtering interface for routing self-alignment across layers.
    \item We propose ReSync, a lightweight timestep-conditioned transition predictor that amortizes this interface over sampled layer pairs with a compositional consistency objective.
    \item We validate ReSync through generation benchmarks and targeted ablations, analyzing whether transition prediction, compositional consistency, and predictor sharing improve both sample quality and internal representations; skipped-depth inference is left as an exploratory extension.
\end{itemize}
