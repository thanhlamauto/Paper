\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Diffusion and flow models have become an effective paradigm for high-fidelity image generation~\citep{ho2020ddpm,song2021sde,dhariwal2021diffusion,lipman2022flow}. Recent Diffusion Transformer (DiT) architectures further improve scalability and generative quality by combining diffusion modeling with the strong sequence modeling capability of transformers~\citep{peebles2023scalable,ma2024sit}. Despite these advances, training diffusion transformers remains computationally intensive, motivating growing interest in methods that improve representation learning to accelerate optimization and enhance generative performance~\citep{yu2024repa,jiang2025sra,haghighi2026layersync,wang2025dispersive}.

A prominent direction is representation alignment (RA) using semantically rich features extracted from external representation models (ERMs) such as DINOv2 or JEPA-style encoders~\citep{yu2024repa,oquab2023dinov2,chen2024jepa}. While these approaches have demonstrated strong empirical performance, they introduce additional computational overhead and inherit limitations from the ERMs, including their inductive biases and representation quality. An alternative line of work instead leverages the hierarchical structure of diffusion transformers by aligning representations across different network depths~\citep{jiang2025sra,haghighi2026layersync}. Compared with external representation alignment methods, these internal approaches are more self-contained and computationally efficient, but they depend heavily on manually selected layer pairs for supervision.

Internal representation alignment remains fundamentally challenging because activations at different transformer depths exhibit substantial semantic discrepancies. Shallow layers primarily capture local patterns and low-level structural information, whereas deeper layers encode increasingly abstract semantic concepts~\citep{mukhopadhyay2024,kim2025,stracke2025,raghu2021visiontransformers,gromov2024unreasonable}. Consequently, directly matching hidden activations across depth is inherently difficult, causing existing methods to rely on heuristic, architecture-dependent layer selection strategies that often do not generalize across model scales or architectures. Moreover, even methods that introduce learnable predictors to bridge the semantic gap between layers are generally designed for a specific source--target layer pair~\citep{jiang2025sra,haghighi2026layersync}. Since the semantic relationship varies substantially across different layer combinations, predictors trained on one pair often transfer poorly to others.

To address these limitations, we propose \emph{LARA}, a novel RA framework that enables flexible alignment across \emph{arbitrary} layer pairs within a diffusion transformer. Our method is motivated by the observation that intermediate activations across network depth simultaneously contain shared structural regularities and layer-specific semantic variations. Building on this insight, LARA learns a lightweight layer-aware transition predictor that maps representations between different depths. The predictor captures common structural information through shared parameters while adapting to varying semantic gaps via conditioning on the source and target layer indices.

Unlike prior methods, LARA is both layer-agnostic and architecture-agnostic. By training over a distribution of layer pairs rather than a single selected pair, our framework removes the need for handcrafted layer selection and naturally supports alignment across the full transformer hierarchy. A uniform pair law already provides a strong baseline; we further introduce layer-pair sampling strategies that improve alignment quality while requiring minimal human intervention. Moreover, by distilling shared semantic structure across layers into a single compact predictor, LARA learns more robust and transferable representations that generalize effectively to downstream tasks.

We extensively evaluate LARA across multiple diffusion transformer architectures and benchmarks. Experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements in both generative quality and representation quality compared with prior methods, while introducing only minimal computational overhead. Comprehensive ablation studies further validate the effectiveness of layer-aware conditioning and shared transition modeling. Together, our results show that LARA provides an effective, scalable, and flexible framework for improving representation learning in diffusion transformers.
