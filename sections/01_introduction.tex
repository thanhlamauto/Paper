\section{Introduction}

Denoising generative models, including diffusion models~\citep{ho2020ddpm,song2019score,song2021sde} and flow matching models~\citep{lipman2022flow}, have become a dominant approach for high-fidelity generation. Transformer-based backbones such as DiT and SiT further improve scalability, but they also make training efficiency a central bottleneck~\citep{peebles2023scalable,ma2024sit}. This has motivated a growing line of work that seeks to improve diffusion transformers by improving the representations they learn during training.

Recent studies show that intermediate representations are closely tied to both optimization and generation quality~\citep{mukhopadhyay2024,kim2025,stracke2025,yu2024repa}. A successful strategy is to align diffusion-transformer hidden states with semantic features from external teachers, such as DINOv2 or large vision-language models~\citep{yu2024repa,wang2025haste,leng2025repae,wang2026vaerepa,oquab2023dinov2,wang2024qwen2vl}. More recent analysis in iREPA suggests that preserving spatial structure can be important beyond global semantic alignment, and reports improvements to REPA-style training by modifying the projection and normalization used for alignment~\citep{singh2025irepa}. While effective, this paradigm depends on additional pretrained models, adds training-time overhead, and may inherit teacher-specific biases in domains where strong external encoders are unavailable or mismatched.

These limitations motivate self-contained guidance, where the diffusion transformer supplies its own training signal. This direction is supported by two observations. First, representation quality is heterogeneous across depth: some intermediate layers carry substantially more useful semantic information than others~\citep{mukhopadhyay2024,kim2025,stracke2025}. Second, external representation guidance often provides stronger benefits when applied to weaker or earlier parts of the network, suggesting that internal supervision can be most valuable when it improves underdeveloped representations~\citep{yu2024repa,wang2025haste}. Together, these observations suggest that the model's stronger internal states can provide useful guidance for its weaker ones, without requiring an external encoder.

Several recent methods pursue this goal, including internal diversity regularization~\citep{wang2025dispersive,yang2026diversedit} and self-alignment methods such as SRA and LayerSync~\citep{jiang2025sra,haghighi2026layersync}. A notable pattern is that REPA- and SRA-style alignment introduces a projection head between source and target features, whereas LayerSync directly aligns selected internal layers. We hypothesize that projection heads may act as soft adapters for statistical mismatch between representation spaces, including differences in scale, variance, channel basis, rank, timestep dependence, and layer-specific computation. However, these adapters are usually local to selected alignment pairs, and existing self-alignment methods still require heuristic choices of target layers, timesteps, projection heads, and loss weights. Despite reducing the need for external encoders, self-contained methods also leave a noticeable gap to external representation guidance in the settings where REPA-style alignment remains strongest~\citep{yu2024repa}.

We propose \emph{ReSync}, a lightweight self-distillation objective that regularizes diffusion transformers through internal representation dynamics rather than direct feature matching. Building on the hypothesis that projection heads may partially reduce statistical mismatch, ReSync treats projection-based alignment as a transition-learning problem. It learns a shared conditional transition model across layer and timestep pairs, avoiding pair-specific alignment shortcuts while preserving layer-specific roles. A compositional consistency constraint encourages long transitions to agree with compositions of shorter transitions, providing a directed internal learning signal without external encoders or additional data. ReSync introduces only a small auxiliary predictor and can optionally support layer-skipping through output distillation.

Our main contributions are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=0.25em,itemsep=0.15em,parsep=0pt]
    \item We reinterpret projection-based alignment as learning transitions between internal representation spaces, rather than directly matching selected features.
    \item We introduce ReSync, a self-contained transition predictor conditioned on layer indices and denoising timestep, requiring no external encoder or additional data.
    \item We enforce compositional consistency across depth, so long-range representation changes agree with compositions of shorter internal transitions.
\end{itemize}
